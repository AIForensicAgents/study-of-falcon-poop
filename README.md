# Study of Falcon Poop (study-of-falcon-poop)

A lighthearted — yet scientifically minded — repository documenting observations of falcon scat (droppings, feces) for educational and field‑study purposes. This README is intended to be clear, professional, and a little fun: because science can have a sense of humor, even when the subject doesn’t.

---

## Overview

This project collects photographic records, morphological observations, and contextual notes about falcon droppings gathered during field surveys. The goal is to support educational outreach, natural history curricula, and non-invasive ecological inference (diet, health, territory use) from scat evidence.

Key goals:
- Build an annotated visual reference for common falcon scat morphologies.
- Provide contextual notes to help infer diet and habitat use.
- Share best practices for ethical, safe observation and documentation.

---

## Why it matters

Falcons, as apex aerial predators, provide valuable information about ecosystem health. Scat-based studies are:
- Non-invasive: collect information without handling animals.
- Cost-effective: photography and simple macroscopic notes can reveal prey composition, seasonal diet shifts, and environmental contaminants.
- Educational: great for outreach and teaching basic field biology and natural history observation skills.

Evidence from scat contributes to conservation planning, urban wildlife management, and long-term monitoring programs.

## Morphology notes

Observational traits commonly recorded:
- **Size & shape**: linear streaks, compact pellets, or mixed deposits; indicates defecation posture and substrate interaction.
- **Coloration**: fresh scat commonly shows **white urate** (uric acid) with darker fecal centers; color variation may indicate diet or gut transit.
- **Texture**: paste-like vs. fibrous vs. particulate — fibers and hair often indicate mammalian prey; bone fragments and exoskeletons indicate birds or arthropods.
- **Embedded material**: feathers, hair, bone fragments, insect parts, or seeds; useful for prey ID without invasive sampling.

Pro tip:
> When in doubt, photograph multiple angles and include a scale (coin or ruler) to improve later measurement and comparison.

## Habitat / diet clues

Scat can yield direct and indirect habitat/diet clues. Interpretations should be cautious and corroborated with other evidence.

Useful indicators:
- **Feathers and bone fragments**: points to avian or small vertebrate prey; feather type helps ID prey species.
- **Hair and rodent bones**: suggests terrestrial rodent prey common near grasslands, parks, or agricultural areas.
- **Insect parts**: may indicate seasonal insectivory or opportunistic feeding.
- **Seeds or plant matter**: often secondary (e.g., from stomach contents of prey) but can provide habitat clues.
- **Urate volume and color**: may reflect hydration, diet composition, or metabolic state.

Caveat: Small-sample inference is speculative. Combine scat evidence with observational data (sightings, nesting sites, hunting behavior) for robust conclusions.

## Ethics / Safety

Safety and ethics are paramount. Observing and documenting wildlife scat must avoid harm to animals, people, and habitats.

Do:
- Observe at a distance; **do not disturb** nesting or roosting birds.
- Wear disposable **gloves** when handling samples or any substrate you may have touched.
- Use hand sanitizer or wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork.
- Avoid creating aerosols (do not sniff or blow on samples).
- Label photos and notes with date, time, GPS coordinates (or general location), and observer name.
- Obtain necessary permits for systematic sampling in protected areas.

Do not:
- Attempt to capture, handle, or restrain falcons.
- Remove large numbers of samples from a site if doing so would alter habitat or disturb animals.
- Transport fecal samples across regions without appropriate biosafety and permitting procedures.

Legal / institutional constraints:
- Check local wildlife regulations and institutional biosafety guidelines before collecting or transporting biological material.

Planned directions to enhance the repository:
